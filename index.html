Intro To Sharks

  • Fins:Sharks have different types of fins; hence, they are characterized by dorsal fins (on the back), pectoral fins (on the sides), pelvic fins (on the underside), anal fins (near the tail), and caudal fins (the tail fin).
    • Fins allow sharks to control balance and movement and also help them in swimming.
  • Gills:Sharks have slits on both their sides of heads which are responsible for extracting oxygen from water.Water goes into the shark's mouth and proceeds through the gills, where oxygen is taken-in and carbon dioxide is given off.
  • Teeth:Sharks have several rows of teeth with periodic replacement during their lifetime.Their teeth are sharp and serrated, which really help them catching and eating their prey.
  • Skin: The surface of shark skin is covered by small tooth-shaped scales hair-like denticles.The skin denticles of sharks act in two ways: they reduce drag in the water and also provide protection to the shark's skin.
  • Skeleton: Cartilage is the material that sharks are built from, making their skeletons very light and flexible.It is its cartilaginous skeleton that makes sharks more flexible than fish with bone skeletons and it helps them move easily through the water.

  • Each species is classified into a genus and family based on characteristics such as body shape, spine, snout, and fin types. The great white shark, for instance, has two dorsal fins: one huge one around the center of its body and the other behind. Its mouth is located behind its eyes. In contrast, an angel shark has a flat nose with the mouth underneath and a pair of fins in the dorsal region close to the tail.
Shark behaviour Sharks' evolutionary traits have shaped not only how they seem but also how they have adapted to live in the wild. Based on their living environment, which is intimately linked to the structure and functions of their bodies, sharks can be divided into three categories. There are three types of sharks in the ocean: those that live at the surface and move slowly, those that are in the pelagic zone and can swim incredibly well, and those that stay near the seafloor, or benthic zone, and move slowly in quest of food.
Shark Average Weight
  • Whale sharks: The largest fish species is the whale shark, which weighs an average of 20.6 metric tons (45,000 pounds). The largest people have a maximum weight of 34 metric tons, or 75,000 pounds.
  • Great White Shark: Female great white sharks are larger than males, and adult sharks typically weigh between 680 and 1,800 kilograms (1,500 and 4,000 pounds).
  • Tiger Shark: Female tiger sharks are larger than male tiger sharks, with an average weight of 385 to 635 kilograms (850 to 1,400 pounds).
  • Hammerhead Shark: The weight of hammerhead sharks can range from 90 to 450 kilograms (200 to 1,000 pounds), depending on the species.
Some Amazing Facts
  • Sharks can be found all across the planet, from cold oceans to balmy, tropical lagoons. Some even live in rivers and freshwater lakes!
  • Sharks swim with less energy because of their fusiform body shape, which is spherical and tapering at both ends.
  • Sharks consume much less food than most people realize. Compared to warm-blooded creatures, cold-blooded animals have a substantially slower metabolism. In actuality, a shark in a zoological setting consumes between 1 and 10% of its body weight every week. Analyses conducted on wild sharks reveal comparable dietary habits.
  • Out of around 350 shark species, only 32 have ever been reported to attack humans. Like most natural creatures, sharks prefer to stay away from humans. Shark attacks are likely the result of their mistaking humans for food or defending their territory.
  • Sharks frequently lose teeth, especially during feeding, and unlike bony fish, their teeth are not fixed in their jaws. Because sharks have three or more rows of teeth, a lost tooth is promptly replaced by another. A single shark's teeth can total up to 30,000 during its lifetime.

Prey and Predator: Sharks

Great White Shark

The great white shark (Carcharodon carcharias) is one of the most iconic predators of the ocean. With its powerful jaws and keen senses, it hunts seals, sea lions, and various fish species.

Prey

  • Seals
  • Fish
  • Sea lions

Predators

  • Orcas

Hammerhead Shark

The hammerhead shark (Sphyrnidae) is instantly recognizable by its unique hammer-shaped head, known as the cephalofoil. It feeds on a variety of prey including fish, stingrays, and smaller sharks.

Prey

  • Fish
  • Stingrays
  • Smaller sharks

Predators

  • Orcas
  • Large sharks

Tiger Shark

The tiger shark (Galeocerdo cuvier) earns its name due to the dark vertical stripes resembling a tiger's pattern when young. It is a highly opportunistic feeder, preying on a wide range of marine life.

Prey

  • Turtles
  • Seabirds
  • Dolphins

Predators

  • Orcas
  • Large sharks

Leopard Shark

The leopard shark (Triakis semifasciata) is a beautiful and relatively harmless species found in shallow coastal waters. It primarily feeds on small fish, crustaceans, and mollusks.

Prey

  • Small fish
  • Crustaceans
  • Mollusks

Predators

  • No major predators

Whale Shark

The whale shark (Rhincodon typus) holds the title of being the largest fish in the ocean. Despite its enormous size, it is a filter feeder, consuming plankton and small fish by swimming with its wide mouth open.

Prey

  • Plankton
  • Small fish

Predators

  • No major predators

Counting shark populations accurately presents a considerable challenge due to various factors such as their wide-ranging migration patterns, diverse habitats, and the elusive nature of many species. Sharks inhabit a wide range of marine environments, from shallow coastal waters to the depths of the open ocean, making them difficult to survey comprehensively.

Furthermore, many shark species exhibit migratory behavior, moving across vast distances in response to changes in temperature, prey availability, and other environmental factors. This mobility adds complexity to population assessments as sharks may not remain in one location long enough to be accurately counted. Additionally, the vastness of the ocean and the limited accessibility of certain regions pose logistical challenges to researchers attempting to study and quantify shark populations. Traditional survey methods such as visual sightings, tagging, and acoustic monitoring have their limitations, particularly in deep-sea habitats where sharks are less frequently encountered.

Conservation Efforts

Amidst threats like overfishing, habitat loss, and climate change, dedicated global efforts prioritize the conservation of sharks. Strategies encompass a range of initiatives, including the establishment of marine protected areas (MPAs) to safeguard vital habitats and mitigate exploitation pressures. Sustainable fishing practices, such as catch limits and shark finning bans, help prevent population declines while ensuring fisheries' sustainability. Additionally, educational outreach programs play a crucial role in raising awareness about shark conservation, dispelling myths, and fostering empathy towards these apex predators.

By engaging communities and stakeholders worldwide, these efforts promote a culture of stewardship and inspire action to protect sharks and marine ecosystems. International collaboration further strengthens conservation endeavors, facilitating knowledge exchange, resource sharing, and coordinated action across borders. Through collective dedication and cooperation, we can mitigate threats to shark populations and ensure their continued presence, preserving the balance and biodiversity of our oceans for generations to come.
